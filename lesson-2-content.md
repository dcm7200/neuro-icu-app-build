# Module 5, Lesson 2: Focused Exam in the Unconscious or Sedated Patient

## Overview
The neurological examination of an unconscious or sedated patient requires modification from the standard awake exam. This lesson focuses on the coma exam—a streamlined, systematic approach to assess the unconscious patient's neurological status.

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Perform a focused neurological exam in sedated/unconscious patients
- Assess level of consciousness using standardized scales
- Evaluate pupillary function and brainstem reflexes
- Interpret motor and reflex findings
- Recognize patterns that suggest localization of injury

---

## What is the Coma Exam?

The **coma exam** is a streamlined neurological assessment for patients who cannot cooperate or respond to commands. It prioritizes:
- **Consciousness level** (arousal, responsiveness)
- **Brainstem function** (pupils, eye movements, gag reflex)
- **Motor function** (tone, posture, response to stimuli)
- **Reflexes** (deep tendon, pathological)

Unlike the full neuro exam, you cannot assess cognition, cranial nerve function fully, or cerebellar signs in an unconscious patient.

---

## Part 1: Level of Consciousness

### Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
The GCS is the gold standard for documenting consciousness level in comatose patients.

**Three components:**
- **Eye Opening (E):** 1-4 points
  - 4 = Spontaneous
  - 3 = To verbal command
  - 2 = To pain
  - 1 = No response

- **Verbal Response (V):** 1-5 points
  - 5 = Oriented, converses normally
  - 4 = Disoriented conversation
  - 3 = Inappropriate words
  - 2 = Incomprehensible sounds
  - 1 = No response

- **Motor Response (M):** 1-6 points
  - 6 = Obeys commands
  - 5 = Localizes to pain
  - 4 = Withdraws from pain
  - 3 = Abnormal flexion (decorticate)
  - 2 = Abnormal extension (decerebrate)
  - 1 = No response

**Total GCS = E + V + M (range 3–15)**

## Part 2: Brainstem Function

### Pupillary Assessment
- **Size:** Normal 2-4mm; measure with light and dark
- **Reactivity:** Should constrict briskly to light
- **Symmetry:** Normally equal

**Abnormal patterns:**
- **Pinpoint pupils:** Pontine hemorrhage or severe opioid overdose
- **Mid-fixed pupils:** Midbrain dysfunction (increased ICP)
- **Blown pupil (one side):** Uncal herniation (medical emergency)
- **Sluggish pupils:** Metabolic encephalopathy, diffuse brain injury

### Oculocephalic Reflex ("Doll's Eyes")
*Only perform if no cervical spine injury suspected*

- Turn patient's head side-to-side
- Normal response: Eyes move opposite to head rotation (intact brainstem)
- Absent response: Eyes stay fixed in orbit (brainstem dysfunction)

### Corneal Reflex
- Gently touch cornea with cotton swab
- Normal: Both eyes blink (trigeminal + facial nerve intact)
- Absent: Suggests brainstem or peripheral nerve injury

### Gag Reflex
- Stimulate back of pharynx with tongue depressor
- Normal: Pharyngeal contraction
- Absent: Suggests vagal nerve (CN X) dysfunction

---

## Part 3: Motor Examination

### Tone Assessment
- **Flaccid:** No resistance to passive movement (spinal shock, severe brainstem injury)
- **Normal:** Slight resistance
- **Rigid:** Increased tone throughout (indicates upper motor neuron involvement)
- **Spastic:** Velocity-dependent increase in tone

### Posturing
- **Decorticate (flexor posturing):** Arms flexed, legs extended → suggests cerebral hemispheric injury
- **Decerebrate (extensor posturing):** All extremities extended → suggests midbrain/brainstem injury
- **Flaccid:** No posturing → spinal cord injury or severe brainstem injury

### Motor Response to Pain
Stimulus hierarchy:
1. Nail bed pressure (painful stimulus)
2. Supraorbital pressure
3. Sternal rub

**Responses:**
- Withdrawal: Limb pulls away from stimulus (good sign)
- Decoricate/decerebrate posturing: See above
- No response: Worst prognostic sign

---

## Part 4: Reflex Assessment

### Deep Tendon Reflexes (DTRs)
- **Brisk (++++):** Upper motor neuron lesion
- **Normal (++):** Baseline
- **Diminished (+):** Lower motor neuron or nerve injury
- **Absent (0):** Severe LMN injury or spinal shock

### Pathological Reflexes
- **Babinski sign:** Extensor plantar response (upgoing toe) → upper motor neuron lesion
- **Clonus:** Rhythmic jerking in response to stretch → indicates hyperreflexia

---

## Part 5: Systematic Approach to the Coma Exam

**Use this framework:**

1. **Stabilize cervical spine** (if trauma suspected)
2. **Assess responsiveness**
   - Call patient's name
   - Apply painful stimulus (nail bed)
3. **Check pupils and brainstem reflexes**
   - Pupil size, reactivity, symmetry
   - Oculocephalic reflex (if no C-spine risk)
4. **Assess motor function**
   - Check tone in all extremities
   - Note posturing
   - Test response to pain
5. **Test reflexes and Babinski**
6. **Document GCS score**

---

## Interpretation: What the Exam Tells You

### GCS and Prognosis
- **GCS 3-5:** Very severe, often irreversible damage
- **GCS 6-8:** Severe, significant morbidity expected
- **GCS 9-12:** Moderate, variable outcomes
- **GCS 13-15:** Mild, generally better prognosis

### Brainstem Signs
- **Intact brainstem:** Pupils reactive, doll's eyes present, gag reflex intact
- **Brainstem dysfunction:** Pupillary abnormalities, absent reflexes → urgent neurology consult

### Motor Patterns
- **Localizing response:** Better prognosis (patient still processing stimuli)
- **Decorticate posturing:** Cerebral injury, but some brainstem function
- **Decerebrate posturing:** Brainstem injury, worse prognosis
- **Flaccid:** Worst prognosis

## Key Takeaways

✅ The coma exam is a **simplified, systematic** approach for unconscious patients  
✅ **GCS** is the standard tool for documenting consciousness  
✅ **Brainstem signs** (pupils, reflexes) help localize injury  
✅ **Motor responses** indicate severity and prognosis  
✅ **Serial exams** matter more than one-time assessment—track changes  
✅ When in doubt, **escalate**: contact neurology or neurosurgery  

---

## Clinical Pearl

> The coma exam changes over time. What matters most is **trend**. A patient improving from GCS 5 to 7 is encouraging; deterioration from 12 to 10 warrants urgent investigation.

Always compare to prior exams and note time of day (sedation wear-off vs. true change).
